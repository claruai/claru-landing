# Investor Pitch Deck

## When to use this approach
Series A/B fundraising. Demo day presentations. Any context where you need to convince investors to fund your vision.

## Core philosophy
The deck is not a business plan compressed into slides. It's a story about an inevitable future, and why this team is the only one who can build it. Every slide sustains belief long enough to get to the next slide and ultimately, the next meeting. At Series A+, investors have seen thousands of decks — novelty of the idea matters less than the sharpness of the narrative and credibility of the evidence.

## Narrative arc (Sequoia format)
1. **Company purpose** — One sentence. Not a tagline. A declaration of what changes if you succeed.
2. **Problem** — Make the investor feel the pain. Data supports emotion, not the other way around. Must feel urgent, large, and currently unsolved.
3. **Solution** — What you do, in benefits not features. One slide. Most founders use three when one is right.
4. **Why now** — What changed (regulatory, technical, behavioral) that makes this the only moment this works? A strong "why now" separates a business from a timing bet.
5. **Market size** — TAM/SAM/SOM. Build from first principles, not Gartner reports. Show the math.
6. **Competition** — 2x2 matrix on axes that favor you. Be honest about who's big. Explain why you win anyway.
7. **Product** — Screenshots or demo video. Proves the solution is real.
8. **Business model** — How you make money. Pricing, unit economics, gross margin.
9. **Traction** — The most important slide at Series A. Revenue, growth rate, retention. Charts go up and to the right. Annotate inflection points.
10. **Team** — Why these specific humans are uniquely positioned to win. Relevant experience only.
11. **The ask** — How much, what it buys (18-24 month runway), what milestones you'll hit. Be specific.

## Visual approach
- Clean, confident, minimal. Investors read twenty decks a week. Clarity is competitive advantage.
- One idea per slide. One chart per slide. One chart that makes one point.
- Consistent typography and color throughout. Inconsistency signals execution risk.
- Traction charts should be large and readable.
- Dark or white — pick one. Mixing signals design immaturity.

## Common mistakes
- **Feature dump on the solution slide** — Say what you do in one sentence, then show it.
- **Weak "why now"** — "The market is growing" is not a reason. Find the specific inflection.
- **Sandbagging on competition** — "No direct competitors" signals you haven't done homework.
- **TAM from a Gartner report** — Build market size from first principles.
- **The ask buried at the end** — State the raise amount clearly. Ambiguity is a red flag.

## How it differs from a McKinsey deck
McKinsey answers "what should we do?" based on analysis. Pitch decks answer "what will the world look like?" based on vision. McKinsey uses footnotes for rigor. Pitch decks use traction curves and team credibility. One is inward-looking (answer the client's question). The other is outward-looking (pull the investor into a possible future).
