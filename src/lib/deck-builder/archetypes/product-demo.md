# Product Demo Deck

## When to use this approach
Showing a product in action to a prospect who already agreed to a demo. Not a pitch, not a feature list — a guided experience where the product is the protagonist.

## Core philosophy
The prospect doesn't care about your features. They care about what their life looks like if they use this. Every slide should either establish pain, build context, or reveal a moment of relief. You're not asking for money or belief — you're asking for attention and imagination.

## Narrative arc
1. **Mirror the prospect** — One slide showing you understand their situation. No company logos. "Here's what we understand about your world."
2. **Name the specific friction** — Not generic pain. The exact moment of frustration. "You're spending 4 hours a week in spreadsheets reconciling data your CRM already has."
3. **Frame the demo as a journey** — "Let me show you a day in the life of your team six months from now."
4. **Walkthrough in scenarios, not features** — Structure as: "Sarah on your design team needs to do X. Here's how she does it." Use personas tied to their actual org.
5. **One wow moment per use case** — Every scenario should have a single reveal that's genuinely surprising. Don't bury it.
6. **Handle the comparison moment** — One slide that honestly shows where you differ. Not a feature grid. A philosophy statement.
7. **Social proof at the moment of doubt** — Not at the end. Slip in a customer quote exactly when they're thinking "does this actually work?"
8. **Specific next step** — Not "any questions?" but "based on what you saw today, here are three ways teams like yours get started."

## Visual approach
- Heavy on product screenshots and screen recordings, edited tightly. Crop to show only what matters.
- Annotation layers on screenshots — circles, callouts, arrows to guide the eye.
- Avoid generic stock photography. Use real product UI.
- Motion and transitions should mirror the product's own feel.
- Large whitespace, single focal point per slide.
- Consider interactive prototype embeds or live demos over static screenshots.

## Common mistakes
- **The harbor tour** — Showing every feature in sequence without a narrative thread. Prospect checks out by slide 6.
- **Starting with company history** — Nobody cares about your founding story during a demo.
- **Generic personas** — Using "Marketing Mary" when the prospect is a VP of Engineering.
- **Death by feature parity** — Listing everything instead of the three things that solve this prospect's exact problem.
- **Saving the next step for the end** — By then you've lost momentum. Seed it earlier.

## How it differs from a McKinsey deck
A McKinsey deck leads with the answer and argues. A demo deck builds an experience and seduces. McKinsey decks are read. Demo decks are felt. McKinsey optimizes for logical completeness. Demo decks optimize for emotional resonance and forward momentum.
