# Case Study / Customer Story

## When to use this approach
Proving ROI to prospects. Customer success stories. Any context where you need to build trust through a real example of your product/service delivering results.

## Core philosophy
The prospect is the mirror, not the audience. The entire deck should make them see themselves in your customer's story. "That's exactly our situation" by slide three, "we need to talk" by the final slide. The customer is the hero, not your product. Your product is the tool the hero used to win. The moment you make your product the protagonist, the case study becomes a brochure.

## Narrative arc
1. **Customer headline** — Company name, logo, one sentence about who they are. Audience immediately locates themselves ("are we like them?").
2. **The situation before** — Specific, concrete. Use the customer's own language. Quotes are more powerful than paraphrase.
3. **The specific problem** — Not a category problem. "They were manually reconciling 40,000 records per week across three systems with a team of five."
4. **Why they chose you** — The evaluation story. What alternatives did they consider? Why did you win?
5. **The implementation** — How long, how many people, what did onboarding look like. Address deployment risk directly.
6. **The results** — Three to four hard metrics. Before/after bar charts outperform text.
7. **Business impact, in their words** — Direct quote from a named executive. The credibility anchor.
8. **What's next** — Customer is expanding, deepening. Signals long-term value.
9. **Bridge to the prospect** — "Based on what we understand about your environment, here's what we'd expect for your team."

## Visual approach
- Real customer photography. Actual people, actual workspace. Stock photography destroys credibility in case studies.
- Big numbers, bold typography. "4 hours to 11 minutes" should be the largest text on the slide.
- Before/after charts. Simple bar chart with two bars is instantly readable.
- Quote callouts with real attribution — name, title, company. Not "a senior leader at a Fortune 500."
- Minimal text. The presenter tells the story; the slide provides evidence.

## Common mistakes
- **Vague metrics** — "Significantly improved productivity" is unusable. Get specific numbers.
- **Making your company the hero** — Every slide about your product instead of the customer's journey weakens trust.
- **Wrong customer profile** — Enterprise case study for an SMB prospect. Relatability is everything.
- **Skipping the evaluation** — Prospects want to know how others made the decision.
- **No executive quote** — Working-level testimonials have low credibility with buying committees.

## How it differs from a McKinsey deck
McKinsey case studies justify a recommendation analytically. Customer story decks transfer belief emotionally. McKinsey starts with the finding. Case studies build forward through narrative. One is for analytical skeptics. The other is for risk-averse buyers who need permission to believe.
