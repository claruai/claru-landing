# Sales Pitch Deck

## When to use this approach
First calls with prospects. Outbound sales meetings. When you need to convince someone your solution solves their problem and they should take the next step.

## Core philosophy
The audience doesn't care about you — they care about their problem. Start with their world, not yours. Make the status quo feel unacceptable before presenting your solution. Every slide should advance a single argument toward one clear call to action.

## Narrative arc
The best sales decks follow a tension-and-release structure:

1. **Hook** — A bold claim about THEIR industry/world that creates curiosity. Not about your company. Something they'll nod along to or be provoked by.
2. **The problem** — Name the specific pain. Quantify the cost (wasted time, lost revenue, failed projects). Make it visceral.
3. **Why now** — What changed? New regulation, market shift, technology inflection point. Create urgency.
4. **The old way vs the new way** — Contrast the broken status quo with the better approach. Don't name your product yet — just the methodology.
5. **Your solution** — Now introduce yourself. Lead with the approach, not the product. How you solve it differently.
6. **Proof** — Case studies, metrics, logos, quotes. Be specific: "23% fewer iterations" beats "improved performance."
7. **How it works** — Remove friction. Show the clear 3-5 step path from first call to production. Make engagement feel low-risk.
8. **Call to action** — One clear next step. Not "contact us" — something specific like "Let's scope a pilot project this week."

## Visual approach
- Clean and uncluttered. Lots of breathing room.
- One message per slide — if someone only reads the titles, they should get the full argument.
- Big numbers for proof points. Stats should be the visual anchor, not buried in text.
- Minimal text. Bullet fragments, not sentences. Bold the key phrase in each point.
- Dark backgrounds work well for premium/tech positioning. Light for enterprise/corporate.
- Use the prospect's own language/terminology when possible.

## Common mistakes
- Starting with "About Us" — nobody cares yet. Earn their attention first.
- Feature lists instead of outcomes — "We have X" is weaker than "You get Y."
- Too many slides on your solution, not enough on the problem — the problem is what creates urgency.
- Generic claims — "We're the leading..." means nothing. Specificity is credibility.
- No clear CTA — the deck fizzles out instead of driving action.
- Trying to close in the deck — the goal of the first deck is to earn the second meeting.

## The champion test
Your prospect has to re-present this internally to people who haven't met you. Every slide should be skim-able, memorable, and self-explanatory. Bold problem framing + clear solution + one killer proof point is what gets forwarded.
