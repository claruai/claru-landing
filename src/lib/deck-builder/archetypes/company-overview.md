# Company Overview / Culture Deck

## When to use this approach
Recruiting. Partnership conversations. Press and investor relations. Any context where people need to understand who you are, what you believe, and how you work — not what you sell.

## Core philosophy
The culture deck is a self-selection mechanism, not a brand exercise. The purpose is to attract people who genuinely belong and repel people who don't. Netflix's 125-slide culture deck worked because it was radically honest, including the hard parts. "If you're not a high performer who thrives without hand-holding, you'll be miserable here." That specificity was the value.

## Narrative arc
Culture decks don't follow a linear argument. They're thematic:

1. **The mission, with weight** — Not "make the world better." A specific claim about what changes if you succeed. One slide, no hedging.
2. **Who we serve and why it matters** — Ground the culture in real purpose.
3. **What we believe** — Core values stated as operational beliefs. Not "integrity" but "we tell customers things they don't want to hear, immediately."
4. **How we make decisions** — The differentiating section. How you handle disagreement, who has authority, when you move fast vs slow.
5. **How we work** — Remote/in-person, meeting norms, async/sync, documentation culture. Be concrete.
6. **Who thrives here and who doesn't** — Uncomfortable to write, extremely effective. Honest self-description.
7. **How we grow people** — Career development, feedback culture, learning investment.
8. **What we're building toward** — 3-5 year vision. Makes it aspirational.
9. **The team** — Real photos, real stories. Not headshots in a grid.

## Visual approach
- Photography is the dominant medium. Real team, real office/remote setups, real work in progress.
- Manifesto-style typography. Short declarations in large type carry emotional weight.
- No corporate stock imagery. A photo of your actual team eating lunch does more than any graphic.
- Color palettes should feel lived-in, not template-generated.
- Consider long-form written sections. Culture decks can be scroll documents (Notion, GitLab's handbook).

## Common mistakes
- **Generic values** — "Innovation, collaboration, integrity" tells candidates nothing distinctive.
- **Aspirational culture, not actual culture** — Writing what you wish, not what exists. Candidates discover the gap in week two.
- **HR compliance document tone** — Repels the people you want.
- **No hard truths** — The most effective decks include something uncomfortable and specific.
- **Missing operational specifics** — How decisions get made, how performance is evaluated, how conflicts get resolved.

## How it differs from a McKinsey deck
McKinsey decks are built for a single engagement. Culture decks are living documents built to outlast any single reader. McKinsey answers "what should we do?" Culture decks answer "who are we?" McKinsey uses conclusion-first. Culture decks build from values to behaviors to proof.
